Student Productivity & Digital Distraction

An Interactive Visual Exploration

Group 3 — Yilin Hou · Xiuxiu Lei · Ziyi Ye · Jieru Zhang · Juanyu Zhang

Core Productivity Drivers

Which factors most directly influence student productivity? These three charts progress from a single-variable relationship to dose-response effects and finally to a two-variable interaction — revealing how study hours, sleep, and stress independently and jointly drive productivity.

1. Study Hours vs. Productivity Score

Exploring the core relationship between time invested in studying and productivity outcomes.

2. Sleep & Productivity: A Dose-Response Relationship

Examining whether sleep duration has an incremental effect on productivity, and whether stress level moderates that effect.

3. Stress × Study Hours Heatmap

Analyzing the interaction effect of stress and study duration — can heavy studying compensate for high stress?

Digital Behavior & Distraction Mechanisms

Is total screen time the real culprit, or do specific apps matter more? These two charts dissect the same question from macro and micro perspectives — time allocation across productivity tiers versus a direct app-by-app comparison.

4 & 5. Digital Time Budget & The App Paradox

Chart 4 breaks down digital activity proportions by productivity tier. Chart 5 uses small multiples to reveal why overall phone use correlates negatively with productivity while individual apps do not.

Academic Performance & Behavioral Patterns

Does effort actually translate into results? These charts confront the paradox between productivity and grades, and examine whether attendance and assignments are sufficient conditions for high productivity.

6 & 7. The Grade Paradox & Attendance–Assignment Analysis

Chart 6 reveals the near-zero correlation between final grades and productivity. Chart 7 uses a bubble chart to test whether attendance and assignment completion truly convert into productivity gains.

Group Profiles & Personalized Exploration

Moving from observation to action — what does a typical high-productivity student look like? Do age groups differ? And where would you land? These three charts synthesize all dimensions and hand control to the viewer.

8. Student Lifestyle Profiles

Radar chart comparing behavioral profiles across productivity tiers — a holistic portrait of high vs. low productivity lifestyles.

9. Age Group Behavioral Comparison

Testing whether students of different ages exhibit significantly different digital habits, study behaviors, and productivity outcomes.

10. Interactive What-If Simulator

Adjust study hours, sleep, phone usage, and stress to see the predicted productivity distribution of matching students — personalized exploration at your fingertips.
